Story highlights

  • There have been 3 outbreaks of Aviation Feces Fever in Northeastern America in the last month
  • Globally, there are around 500,000 infected by A.F.F. every year

Vital Signs is a monthly program bringing viewers health stories from around the world

(CNN)“It is uncommon to see A.F.F. this far north on the east coast. Outbreaks are usually found in warmer parts of the country, near the tropics.”, said Dr. Teller from the Centers for Disease Control.

Recent A.F.F. outbreaks across the Adirondack region have authorities worried.

The disease is being spread by Chroicocephalus philadelphia and Larus smithsonianus, more commonly known as the Northeast American Gull. Scientists believe the disease began spreading up from the south later this year during migration.

Read: This man's blood saved 2 million babies

There have already been 3 outbreaks in lakes across Northern New York. Two women vacationing in Lake Placid have been infected and are being treated at the Adirondack Medical Center. Outbreaks have also been located in Minerva Lake and Schroon Lake.

Authorities are warning all citizens to be cautious around seagulls and their feces.

Caused by Early Gull Migration

    “Aviation Feces Fever isn’t something most people need to worry about. Make sure to wash your hands if you come in contact with a seagull and you should be fine.” says Sherman Craig, Director of the NYS Adirondack Park Agency. “The disease is only transmitted through open wounds. If you think you have A.F.F. please seek medical attention immediately.”

    Most people with A.F.F. do not realize they have it until it’s too late.

    Dr. Embée Ripp, Centers for Disease Control

    The unfortunate thing about Aviation Feces Fever, is that the symptoms are easily overlooked. “Most people with A.F.F. do not realize they have it until it’s too late.” said Dr. Embée Ripp in an interview last Thursday. “The symptoms start off slow, almost unnoticeable, but after several days they quickly escalate into something much worse.”

    Read: The worms that invade your brain

    Dr. Ripp is warning readers to watch out for A.F.F. symptoms. Symptoms include mild fever, excessive sweating, muscle aches, diarrhea, and fatigue. Sometimes A.F.F. shows itself through a light-pink skin irritation around the infected area.

    “The only way to conclusively determine if someone has A.F.F. is through a blood test. Anyone experiencing these symptoms is advised to seek medical attention quickly. It is better to be safe than sorry.”, says Dr. Ripp.

    “We’ll never fully eradicate it,” says Ripp. “But if we can make citizens aware of the dangers, we can control it as a public health problem.”
